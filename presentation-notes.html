Slide 01 · Cover

Plant Sentinel — “A digital twin that pays for itself.”

Section id s-cover · dark "ink" background.

What this slide is showing

The title page. Black background with a yellow accent. The headline is the product name we gave our work — Plant Sentinel. The tagline reads: “A digital twin that pays for itself.” Below it, the proof teaser: 42 of the last 46 inverter service tickets were visible in the performance data before the ticket was opened, with a median 51.5 days of warning. Two yellow chips at the bottom show the team (SYZ) and what we ran it on (Plant A — 9.4 years × 65 inverters).

Say this

“We’re Team Syz. We built Plant Sentinel — a digital twin for solar plants that earns back its keep. We ran it on Enerparc’s real Plant A data: nine and a half years of five-minute readings from sixty-five inverters. The headline result is that forty-two out of the last forty-six service tickets at this plant were already visible in the performance data before anyone called a technician — on average about seven weeks earlier. That’s the bet of the next ten minutes.”

Why it matters: We’re not pitching a model. We’re pitching a method that already back-tested against real maintenance history at 91% (42/46) hit-rate with seven-week lead time.
If asked
  • What is an “inverter”? The box that converts a string of solar panels’ DC current into AC current the grid can use. A big plant has dozens — here, 65. Each one is a maintenance unit.
  • What does “digital twin” mean in your version? Two layers: (1) a peer comparison — each inverter vs the rest of the plant in real time; (2) an absolute physics baseline — each inverter vs what irradiance and temperature say it should produce. Together they catch both individual faults and plant-wide losses.
  • Why “Plant Sentinel”? Because it watches the plant and raises a flag before the alarm system does. The 51.5-day median lead time is the “sentinel” payoff.
“Let me show you that 42-out-of-46 number on a real chart.”
Slide 02 · The Hook

42 of 46 tickets — flagged in advance

Section id s-hook · dark background. Image: assets/leadtime.png (lead time per ticket).

What this slide is showing

A massive number on the left: 42/46. Underneath, the line “Failures visible in the data first.” A yellow chip shows the median warning before a ticket: 51.5 days. On the right, a chart labelled “Lead time per ticket” — each ticket the operator opened in real life is plotted against how many days earlier our method had already flagged that inverter as underperforming.

Say this

“The plant’s logbook has 46 inverter-specific service tickets in the dataset — real broken capacitors, dead boards, insulation faults. We took our method, ran it blind on the data, and asked: would we have flagged this inverter before the ticket was written? The answer was yes for forty-two of them. The median warning was fifty-one and a half days. That’s a back-test against a real maintenance log, not a forecast.”

Why it matters: 51.5 days is the difference between an emergency truck roll and a planned visit. Planned visits are roughly an order of magnitude cheaper than reactive ones.
Watch out

If anyone asks about the four we missed (46 − 42 = 4): they were partial single-string outages below our 0.8 alert threshold. Our roadmap answer is string-level detection via the DC current signal (I_DC_SUM) — already written up in docs/QA_PREP.md. Don’t pretend the 4 don’t exist.

If asked
  • Is 51.5 days the average or the median? Median. The average would be skewed by a few tickets we caught months in advance. Median is the honest middle.
  • How do you know you’re not just “flagging” every inverter all the time? The method only flags inverters that drop persistently below the peer plant — not transient dips. We filter curtailment (grid-side throttling) so it doesn’t cause false alarms. And the back-test is against tickets the operator actually wrote, so noise wouldn’t produce a 42/46 alignment.
  • What kinds of failures were these 46 tickets? Broken capacitors, control boards, insulation faults — the usual inverter mortality.
  • How did you decide a ticket counted as “flagged”? If our peer-ratio crossed below the alert threshold any time before the ticket date for that specific inverter, it counts as flagged.

Slide 03 · The Problem

Failures their monitoring never caught

Section id s-problem · light background. Image: assets/heatmap.png (monthly peer ratio heatmap).

What this slide is showing

Headline: “Failures their monitoring never caught.” Three black chips spell out the dataset: 9.4 years, 65 inverters, 5-minute readings. The body text restates that — a reading every five minutes from every inverter for almost a decade. The image on the right is a heatmap: rows are inverters, columns are months, colour is each inverter’s peer-ratio. Dark bands = healthy. Bright/light bands = persistent underperformance. The visual point: anyone can see the trouble in the heatmap, but the plant’s own error log never recorded most of it.

Say this

“What we got from Enerparc is huge but simple: every five minutes, for nine and a half years, every one of the sixty-five inverters reports its power output. That’s almost a million rows of data. And what we found in it — this heatmap shows it clearly — is that several inverters spend long stretches significantly underperforming their peers. The error-code log doesn’t mention any of it. The performance data does.”

Why it matters: This is the entire pitch in one image — silent losses sitting in the data for years. The error-code system the plant relies on simply doesn’t see them.
If asked
  • What is “peer ratio”? Just one inverter divided by the plant median at the same five-minute timestamp. 1.0 means “same as everyone else.” 0.5 means “half as much as the typical inverter.” Sun, clouds and seasons hit everyone the same way and cancel out. We explain this on the next slide.
  • Why doesn’t the existing monitoring catch this? Because the existing monitoring is alarm-based — it’s waiting for an inverter to throw an error code. If the inverter is just running quietly at 50% of what it should, no code fires. Several of these failures simply never tripped an alarm.
  • 9.4 years × 65 inverters × five minute samples — how many rows? About 990,000 timestamped records of full plant state. Verified.
“So here’s the trick — the one formula that runs underneath all of this.”
Slide 04 · The Method

Simple on purpose — peer ratio

Section id s-method · light background. Big formula. A red “0.55” example value.

What this slide is showing

A black box with the formula in monospace:

PEER RATIO = INVERTER ÷ PLANT MEDIAN (same timestamp)

To the left, the body text explains: every five minutes, divide each inverter by the median across the plant. Clouds, seasons, rain — they hit every inverter the same way, so they cancel out. 1.0 = healthy. Below 1.0 = something’s wrong. A massive red 0.55 on the right gives an example reading. A panel reminds the eye: “Below 1.0 = trouble.” The body warns about one trap — grid curtailment (the grid operator telling the plant to throttle down) looks exactly like a fault, so we explicitly filter it out.

Say this

“Our core method is deliberately simple. For every five-minute snapshot we divide each inverter by the median of the rest of the plant. Weather, time of day, season — everything that hits all inverters together — cancels out. One-point-zero means healthy. Anything well below one means that one inverter is producing less than its siblings under the same sun. Here, a real example: zero-point-five-five — about half of normal. The one trap we have to handle is when the grid operator orders the plant to curtail output. That looks identical to a fault, so we filter those periods explicitly. Without that filter, you flag healthy inverters all day.”

Why it matters: The method is one line of math. That’s the point — it’s defensible, auditable, and runs on data Enerparc already has.
Watch out

“Curtailment” is jargon. If a non-technical juror is in the room, explain it once: “the grid operator tells the plant to dial down output because the local grid can’t take more — Germany does this often when it’s sunny.” The signals in the data are EVU and DV codes; only ~0.27% of time steps. Small footprint, but if you skip the filter, peer ratio drops uniformly and looks like a fault.

If asked
  • Why median, not mean? Median is robust to the outliers we’re trying to catch. If three inverters are at 50% and 62 are healthy, the mean drags down; the median stays at “healthy” so the broken ones stand out clearly.
  • Doesn’t that mean a uniform fault — say dust on everything — would be invisible? Yes, and that’s exactly the honest limitation. We’ll show how we close it three slides from now with the absolute twin.
  • How sensitive is “below 1.0” — is that the alert threshold? Our actual alert threshold is 0.8 for persistent drops. We use 1.0 in the explanation because it’s the “healthy” reference. The 0.55 example shown here is well into the “definitely broken” zone.
  • Does it work with only 65 inverters? What about a plant with 4? Peer-ratio gets stronger as the peer group grows. For very small plants you fall back on the absolute twin instead — same physics, no peers needed.

Slide 09 · The Twin (Differentiator)

We closed our own limitation — the absolute twin

Section id s-twin · light background. Image: assets/twin_uniform_loss.png (uniform-loss stress test).

What this slide is showing

Headline: “We closed our own limitation.” The argument: peer ratio is relative. If a loss hits every inverter equally (uniform soiling, plant-wide degradation), the peers all move together and the ratio stays at 1.0 — blind. So we also built an absolute twin: each inverter compared to its own healthy baseline, computed from irradiance and module temperature. R² = 0.957. The chart shows a stress test: inject a uniform −10% loss across the whole plant. The peer metric stays at 1.00 (blind). The absolute twin drops from 0.90 to 0.81 (caught). Four cards along the side: plant-wide gap ~7–10%, model fit R² 0.957, bleeding now ~€1.5k/yr, twin section ceiling ~€29k/yr.

Say this

“One honest limitation of the peer method: it’s relative. If a loss hits everyone the same — say uniform dust, or a plant-wide degradation — the peer ratio stays at one-point-zero and we’re blind. So we also built an absolute twin. Each inverter has its own healthy baseline modelled from irradiance and module temperature — basically physics. Predicts healthy output at R-squared zero-point-nine-five-seven. To prove it works, we injected a uniform minus-ten-percent loss across every inverter. The peer metric stayed at one-point-zero — confirmed blind. The absolute twin dropped from zero-point-nine-zero to zero-point-eight-one — caught the common-mode loss. On real data the twin surfaces a plant-wide gap of seven to ten percent. The peer method gives you the per-inverter story; the twin gives you the plant-wide bottom line. Together: no blind spot.”

Why it matters: This is the slide that says “we know what we built, including its weak spot, and we already addressed it.” Juries reward that.
Number bookkeeping

Three money figures live on this slide — keep them straight: €42.3k/yr = the peer-method upper-bound risk for the section 08/09 collapse (slide 7). €1.5k/yr = twin-estimated current bleed on the collapse (close cousin of the €1,200/yr “realised so far”). €29k/yr = twin’s conservative ceiling for that section if uncorrected. The twin number is conservative; the peer number is the upper bound. Different methods, both valid — that’s the point.

If asked
  • What is the actual model? P_expected = k_i × G × (1 + γ × (T − 25)). Where G is irradiance, T is module temperature, γ is the temperature coefficient. k_i is fitted per inverter from clean-state windows. kWp-free — doesn’t depend on the (broken) nameplate register.
  • R² 0.957 — on what data? Held-out clean periods (no tickets, no curtailment, no outages). Per-inverter fits.
  • Why doesn’t a uniform −10% show as 0.90 → 0.80 cleanly? Because the twin baseline itself isn’t a perfect 1.0 — it’s closer to 0.90 in this comparison frame. The drop of ~0.09 matches the injected loss within noise.
  • Why both methods? Pick one. They answer different questions. Peer ratio is great for per-inverter triage — “which one to fix.” Twin is great for plant-wide bookkeeping — “how much is the whole plant losing.” Operations needs both.
  • Where can I see the proof? runs/plant_a/twin/TWIN.md — full validation with numbers.

Reference

Glossary & Numbers Cheatsheet

Skim before you walk on stage. The numbers below cross-checked against the project memory file.

Words to know

  • Inverter — box that turns DC from solar strings into AC for the grid. Plant A has 65.
  • kWp — kilowatts-peak. The rated peak DC capacity of an inverter or string. Important here because 11 entries in the plant register are wrong.
  • Peer ratio — inverter output ÷ plant median output at the same timestamp. 1.0 = healthy. Below = trouble.
  • Curtailment (EVU / DV) — grid operator orders the plant to throttle output. Looks like a fault if you don’t filter it. Only ~0.27% of timesteps but high impact if ignored.
  • Digital twin — our usage: peer-ratio layer (relative) + absolute physics layer (irradiance × temperature) = no blind spots.
  • Irradiance / module temperature — inputs to the absolute twin. Irradiance = how much sun is hitting the panels. Module temperature affects efficiency (panels lose ~0.4%/°C above 25°C — that’s the γ in the formula).
  • ENS — grid undervoltage alarm. Costliest alarm category because real energy is lost when it fires.
  • pvlib — open-source Python solar physics library. Used to validate Plant B’s irradiance sensor against clear-sky theory.
  • SCADA — the plant’s control/monitoring system. Where the five-minute data comes from.

Numbers to never get wrong

  • 42 / 46 tickets flagged in advance. Median 51.5 days = ~7 weeks.
  • 9.4 years × 65 inverters × 5-min ≈ 990,442 rows. Plant A.
  • Sections 01.08.x + 01.09.x collapsing since Aug 2025. 14 inverters, 368 kWp. Realised ~€1,200/yr, €42,325/yr at risk (slide rounds to €42.3k).
  • 840 hours outage / 365 d for the worst single inverter in those sections.
  • Error telemetry dead since Nov 2019 for those sections — that’s why no alarm fired.
  • INV 01.03.018 = ~70% of peers for a year, no ticket. ~€500/yr (independent check) / €432/yr (chart — conservative). README footnote explains the gap.
  • €64,247 plant-wide underperformance with no inverter ticket. Only 7% ticketed.
  • 9,211 raw alarms → 823 incidents. 25% trip / 4% derate / 71% nuisance. ENS the costliest.
  • 11 inverters with stale kWp register (slide visually shows ~8). Example: INV 01.05.030 listed 5.64 kWp, peaks 23 kW.
